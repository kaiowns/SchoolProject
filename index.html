üî± 1. Concept of God

Islam

God is known as Allah, the singular and all-powerful being beyond human comparison. In Islam, Allah is both just and merciful, and believers strive to live in submission to His divine will.

Christianity

Christians worship one God in the form of the Holy Trinity: the Father, the Son (Jesus Christ), and the Holy Spirit. Love and salvation are central to the Christian understanding of God.

Buddhism

Buddhism doesn't emphasize a creator god. Instead, it focuses on enlightenment, achieved through self-awareness, meditation, and ethical living. Some traditions revere celestial Buddhas and Bodhisattvas.

Judaism

God, or Yahweh, is the eternal, indivisible creator in Judaism. He is deeply involved in the affairs of the world and has a covenantal relationship with the Jewish people.

üìö 2. Sacred Books & Texts

Islam

The Quran is the central scripture, believed to be the literal word of God. It is written in classical Arabic and covers guidance on every aspect of life.

Christianity

The Bible is composed of the Old Testament and the New Testament, recording God's relationship with humanity and the teachings of Jesus Christ.

Buddhism

The Buddhist canon includes the Tripitaka (Three Baskets), Mahayana Sutras, and Tibetan texts. They contain teachings of the Buddha and commentary by monks over centuries.

Judaism

The Torah is the foundational text, followed by the Talmud and other writings. Together, they preserve laws, traditions, and centuries of rabbinic thought.

üõê 3. Practices & Rituals

Islam

Muslims follow the Five Pillars: faith (shahada), prayer (salat), charity (zakat), fasting during Ramadan (sawm), and pilgrimage to Mecca (hajj). Daily life integrates constant remembrance of God.

Christianity

Worship includes prayer, Bible reading, sacraments like baptism and communion, and celebration of holy days like Easter and Christmas. Community and service are essential practices.

Buddhism

Key practices include meditation, chanting, ethical living, and monastic discipline. Festivals celebrate enlightenment, and offerings are made at temples to express devotion.

Judaism

Observing the Sabbath, keeping kosher, daily prayers, and following seasonal festivals like Passover are central. Life events are marked with deep-rooted ceremonies.

‚ù§Ô∏è 4. Forgiveness

Islam

Forgiveness is a divine attribute of Allah. Muslims seek forgiveness through prayer, sincere repentance, and making amends. God‚Äôs mercy is emphasized repeatedly in the Quran.

Christianity

Forgiveness is at the heart of Christian faith. Jesus preached love for enemies and the power of divine grace. Confession and repentance open the path to salvation.

Buddhism

Forgiveness is essential for mental clarity and spiritual liberation. Buddhists forgive to free themselves from anger and karma, not necessarily as a divine transaction.

Judaism

Yom Kippur, the Day of Atonement, is dedicated to forgiveness. Jews must seek forgiveness from others before asking it from God, reflecting personal responsibility and community healing.
